INTRODUCTION
Background
	In the rapidly evolving digital era, Electronic Governance (e-Governance) has emerged as a global standard for modern public administration. Across the world, governments are transitioning from traditional, paper-based bureaucracies to sophisticated digital platforms to enhance transparency, accountability, and citizen engagement. This shift is driven by the Fourth Industrial Revolution, where data is considered the new oil, and the ability to process information instantly is synonymous with effective governance [1]. The integration of Information and Communication Technology (ICT) in government operations allows for faster data retrieval, secure record-keeping, and improved responsiveness to public needs, ensuring that no community is left behind in the global march toward digitalization.
	In the context of the Philippines, the push for digital transformation is not merely a trend but a legislative mandate. The passage of Republic Act 11032, also known as the "Ease of Doing Business and Efficient Government Service Delivery Act of 2018," compels all government units to streamline their procedures and adopt automated systems to reduce paperwork [2]. As an archipelagic nation, the Philippines relies heavily on its Local Government Units (LGUs) to deliver essential services to the grassroots level. The Barangay, defined as the smallest political unit in the country, serves as the primary planning and implementing unit of government policies, plans, programs, projects, and activities in the community [3]. It is the first line of defense in maintaining peace and order and the primary point of contact for citizens seeking government assistance.
	However, despite these national mandates and the critical role they play, a significant "digital divide" persists. While major cities in the Philippines have begun adopting "Smart City" technologies, many barangays especially those in provincial and far-flung areas remain tied to obsolete administrative practices. A vast number of these units still lack the necessary financial resources and technical infrastructure to modernize, forcing them to rely on manual handwritten logbooks, physical filing cabinets, and fragmented Excel spreadsheets to manage vital crucial data [4]. These traditional methods are come with risks: paper records are vulnerable to physical damage from natural disasters such as floods or fires, they are prone to human error and redundancy, and they make data retrieval a time-consuming, labor-intensive process that slow down decision-making.
	Locally, this struggle is evident in Barangay Pakiad, a community in the Municipality of Oton, Iloilo. Currently, the barangay administration operates without a centralized information system. The officials rely on manual record-keeping for managing resident profiles, processing business permits, and issuing clearances. This lack of digitization creates a bottleneck in service delivery; simple requests for documents can take longer than necessary due to the time spent searching through physical piles of paper. Furthermore, the absence of a secure, digital database raises concerns regarding data privacy and security, as physical logbooks can be easily misplaced or accessed by unauthorized personnel.
	The problem is further compounded by the lack of a modern mechanism for community engagement, particularly in incident reporting. In times of emergencies or community disputes, residents often have no choice but to physically visit the barangay hall or rely on unverified channels to report issues. This delay in communication can have serious consequences for public safety and disaster response.
	To address these multifaceted inefficiencies, the researchers propose the development of "CommunaLink: Online Barangay Management System." This project aims to bridge the technological gap in Barangay Pakiad by introducing a centralized, cloud-based platform. By migrating from manual logs to a secure Google Cloud SQL database, the system will ensure data integrity, prevent loss, and streamline administrative workflows. Moreover, the integration of an Incident Reporting System using geolocation technology will empower residents to participate actively in community safety, creating a governance model that is not only efficient and transparent but also responsive to the digital needs of the modern Filipino [5].
Rationale
	The transition toward digital governance is a necessity for modern public administration. Despite national mandates like the Ease of Doing Business Act, Barangay Pakiad remains burdened by manual, paper-based workflows that are inherently prone to redundancy, data loss, and administrative bottlenecks. The primary rationale for developing "CommunaLink" is to bridge this digital divide by providing a centralized platform that streamlines operations. By digitizing these processes, the system aims to eliminate the frustration residents feel when processing documents, ultimately restoring efficiency and trust in local governance.
	Furthermore, the system addresses a critical need for public safety and community responsiveness. Traditional incident reporting relies on physical presence or unverified communication, delaying responses during emergencies. By integrating a Geolocation-based Incident Reporting System using the Google Maps API, CommunaLink provides real-time situational awareness. It empowers residents to report hazards or crimes accurately and instantly, transforming them from passive recipients of aid into active participants in community security.
	Finally, the vulnerability of physical records to natural disasters such as floods or fires necessitates a secure, digital transition. Migrating to a Cloud-Based Database ensures that vital constituent data is encrypted, backed up, and protected from calamities, safeguarding the barangay’s institutional memory. Moreover, leveraging the Google Cloud Free Tier infrastructure makes this modernization highly sustainable and cost-effective. This proves that robust, enterprise-level governance solutions can be deployed with minimal capital expenditure, providing a practical and replicable model for other resource-constrained barangays.
